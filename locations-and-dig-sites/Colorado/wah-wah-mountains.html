About Wah Wah Mountains

The Wah Wah Mountains, located in Utah, are a premier destination for rockhounding enthusiasts. Known for their stunning red beryl crystals, these mountains attract visitors from all over the world. The geological history of the Wah Wah Mountains is rich and significant, offering a prime spot for both amateur and experienced rockhounds.

The Wah Wah Mountains span public and private lands, with many accessible areas for rockhounding. Red beryl, also known as bixbite, is the primary gem found here, and it is highly prized for its rarity and vibrant color. In addition to red beryl, visitors can find other minerals such as garnet and topaz.

The mountains' rugged terrain and remote location add to the adventure of gem hunting. The high desert environment presents unique challenges, but the reward of finding a rare red beryl crystal makes it worthwhile. The area's natural beauty, with its striking landscapes and diverse wildlife, enhances the overall experience.

Rockhounding in the Wah Wah Mountains requires preparation and respect for the environment. Visitors should bring sufficient supplies, including water, food, and proper tools. It's also essential to follow local regulations and guidelines to preserve the area's natural resources for future generations.

Red beryl was first discovered in the Wah Wah Mountains in the early 1900s, and since then, it has become one of the most sought-after gemstones in the world. The gem's unique color and rarity make it a valuable find for rockhounds. Mining activities over the years have unearthed some spectacular specimens, enhancing the mountains' reputation among collectors.

The geological conditions in the Wah Wah Mountains create the perfect environment for the formation of red beryl. The interplay of volcanic activity, mineral-rich waters, and specific temperature and pressure conditions contribute to the creation of this rare gem. Rockhounds can learn a lot about geological processes by exploring this region.

In addition to red beryl, the Wah Wah Mountains are home to other fascinating minerals. Garnet and topaz are commonly found, each adding to the diversity of the rockhounding experience. The variety of minerals makes each trip to the Wah Wah Mountains a unique and educational adventure.

The Wah Wah Mountains offer a beautiful and challenging environment for rockhounding. The area is known for its rugged terrain and scenic views, adding to the adventure of gem hunting. The mountains' rich deposits continue to draw collectors and enthusiasts from across the globe, making it a must-visit location for anyone interested in geology and mineralogy.

What Can Be Found At Wah Wah Mountains

At the Wah Wah Mountains, rockhounds can find a variety of gemstones and minerals, including:

  • Red Beryl: The primary attraction, known for its vibrant red color and rarity. Red beryl, also known as bixbite, forms in the volcanic rhyolite rocks of the Wah Wah Mountains. It is one of the rarest gemstones in the world, making any discovery a significant find for rockhounds.
  • Garnet: Often found alongside red beryl, garnet adds to the diversity of minerals in the area. Garnets from the Wah Wah Mountains can range in color from deep red to brownish-red, and they are usually found in metamorphic rocks. Their presence alongside red beryl makes for an exciting rockhounding experience.
  • Topaz: Occasionally found in the area, topaz is a beautiful addition to any rockhound's collection. The topaz crystals found in the Wah Wah Mountains can vary in color, including clear, yellow, and blue hues. These crystals often form in the cavities of volcanic rocks and can be found by careful searching and excavation.
  • Hematite: Adds a beautiful contrast to the other minerals found in the Wah Wah Mountains. Hematite is an iron oxide mineral that is often recognized by its metallic luster and dark silver to black color. It forms in a variety of geological environments and can create stunning contrasts when found alongside the bright reds and other colors of beryl, garnet, and topaz.

Location

The Wah Wah Mountains are located in Utah's West Desert. The exact coordinates are 38.4095° N, 113.2754° W. This area is accessible by car, with several marked trails leading to prime rockhounding spots.

The area is divided into several dig sites, some of which are publicly accessible while others may require permission or a fee to access. It's always best to research the specific area you plan to visit before heading out.

Recommended Gear and Tips

When visiting the Wah Wah Mountains, it's important to come prepared. Here are some recommendations for your trip:

  • Plan to stay for at least a full day to fully explore the area and increase your chances of finding valuable gems.
  • Bring plenty of water, snacks, and sun protection, as the area can get quite hot and there are limited facilities nearby.
  • Wear sturdy shoes and protective gloves to prevent injuries from sharp rocks and tools.
  • Carry tools such as rock hammers, chisels, and safety glasses to safely extract and handle the minerals you find.

Rockhounding Tips For Wah Wah Mountains

Red beryl can often be found in the rhyolite rock formations that are abundant in the Wah Wah Mountains. Look for areas where the rock has weathered and eroded, as this can make it easier to find exposed crystals.

Using a rock hammer, carefully chip away at the rhyolite to reveal potential red beryl crystals. The crystals can range in color from light pink to deep red and are typically found in small cavities or vugs within the rock as pictured here.

Another effective method is to search the ground surface for loose red beryl crystals that have been naturally weathered out of the rock. These can often be found in the sandy areas surrounding the base of the mountains.

Bring a sifter or a small hand-held screen to help separate the crystals from the sand and dirt. This can make it easier to spot the red beryl and other minerals like garnet and topaz.

Other Dig Sites in Utah

Utah is a fantastic destination for rockhounding enthusiasts. With its diverse geological features and rich deposits of various minerals and gemstones, the state offers numerous dig sites that cater to both beginners and experienced rockhounds. From stunning red beryl to vibrant topaz, Utah's dig sites provide an exciting and rewarding experience for all.

Topaz Mountain

Topaz Mountain is a renowned destination for rockhounding enthusiasts, known for its stunning topaz crystals and beautiful scenery.

Dugway Geode Beds

Relatively close to Topaz Mountain, the Dugway Geode Beds are a favorite for Utah rockhounds.

The U-Dig Fossil Quarry is a privately-owned quarry that offers the opportunity to collect well-preserved trilobite fossils.
