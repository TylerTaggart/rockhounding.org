Rocks & Minerals In Rhode Island

Rhode Island rockhounding maps to hunt for crystals, gemstones, minerals, and ores. Rhode Island, the smallest state, offers a unique selection of minerals for rockhounds, including graphite, cumberlandite, and bowenite. Despite its size, the state's diverse geology and coastline provide various collecting opportunities, such as the historic graphite mine at Cranston and the shoreline at Narragansett Bay.

  • Graphite

    Graphite, a naturally occurring form of crystalline carbon, can be found in Rhode Island, particularly in the northwest part of the state. The Cranston Graphite Mine is a notable site for collecting this mineral.

  • Beryl

    Beryl, a popular mineral for collectors, can be found in Rhode Island. Aquamarine and morganite, both varieties of beryl, have been reported in pegmatite deposits throughout the state, particularly in Providence County.

  • Garnet

    Garnet, a group of silicate minerals, can be found in Rhode Island, particularly in the schist and gneiss formations throughout the state. Notable locations include the Diamond Hill Mine and the Newport Neck area.

  • Feldspar

    Feldspar, a group of rock-forming minerals, can be found in pegmatite deposits across Rhode Island. Collectors can find potassium and plagioclase feldspar in areas such as the Diamond Hill Mine and the Snake Den State Park.

  • Tourmaline

    Tourmaline, a popular mineral for collectors, can be found in Rhode Island's pegmatite deposits. Schorl, a black variety of tourmaline, has been reported in the Diamond Hill Mine and other locations throughout the state.

Geology of Rhode Island

Rhode Island's geologic history began over a billion years ago with the formation of ancient rocks during the Precambrian Era. The state experienced several orogenies during the Paleozoic Era, including the Taconic, Acadian, and Alleghenian, which contributed to the formation of the Appalachian Mountains.
